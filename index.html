Abstract

This research examines the transformative impact of aerodynamics on a Formula 1 (F1) car development from the late 1800s to the FIA's establishment in 1950. It highlights the crucial role of aerodynamics, especially in the chassis and undercarriage streamlining, driving manufacturers to refine designs for superior speed and efficiency. Emphasising aerodynamics' multifaceted impact on speed and fuel efficiency, the study analyses F1 car evolution through distinct eras, correlating aerodynamic advancements with racing success. Incorporating insights from Formula 1 aerodynamicist Adrian Newey, the research underscores the delicate balance between innovation and safety in aerodynamics, addressing potential risks. Concluding that aerodynamics is a top factor in F1 car competitiveness, the study anticipates continued evolution. It suggests manufacturers can enhance stability and create faster, streamlined cars by pushing aerodynamic boundaries.

Introduction

Formula 1 has always stood as a fiercely competitive and exhilarating game, sparking the interest of innumerable people with its flawless layout and the annual
improvements made with the aid of manufacturers to revolutionise automobile design. Throughout the span of many many years, the shape, chassis, and components of
Formula 1 automobiles have undergone steady and repeated transformations, all geared toward accomplishing lighter and quicker overall performance. Among these
adjustments, one of the key elements that has substantially contributed to superior efficiency, not just in racing vehicles but additionally in standard vehicles,
is the evolution of aerodynamics in motorised cars.
